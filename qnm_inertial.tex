\begin{abstract}
We extend the Keldysh quasi-normal-mode (QNM) quantization framework
of the companion manuscript to the design and topology optimization of
ultra-compact, on-chip quantum-comb inertial sensors.  The target device
is a freeform nonlinear active nanophotonic structure operating in the
comb-lasing regime with rotational/inertial sensing of an output
free-space comb via spatio-temporal photon-statistics measurements.

We derive: (i)~the Keldysh action for a multi-mode $\chi^{(3)}$
nonlinear active cavity with rotational (Sagnac) coupling; (ii)~the
above-threshold mean-field saddle (a generalised Lugiato--Lefever
soliton-comb solution with classical CW/CCW splitting); (iii)~the
Bogoliubov problem for linear quantum fluctuations around the saddle,
showing each comb-pair $\{+m,-m\}$ reduces to a two-mode squeezed
amplifier with Sagnac splitting; (iv)~the input--output relation
projecting cavity QNM fluctuations onto the free-space output field at
detector pixels $\mathbf{r}_{\rm det}$; (v)~the closed-form classical
Fisher information (FI) of a heterodyne or photon-counting measurement
on the comb-pair output for the Sagnac parameter $\Omega$; and (vi)~the
adjoint gradient of this FI with respect to the four design fields
$\{\varepsilon, g_{\rm gain}, \chi^{(2)}, \chi^{(3)}\}(\mathbf{r})$,
delivering a fully differentiable TopOpt objective.

We benchmark the linearised comb-pair covariance against direct
qutip Lindblad simulation, finding $<1\%$ agreement in the
twin-beam squeezed quadrature variance up to $S=2\chi/\kappa = 0.85$.
A worked example for a GaAs micro-ring at $\lambda=1550\,\mathrm{nm}$,
$Q=10^5$, $R=10\,\mu\mathrm{m}$ gives a Sagnac coupling
$m_p = R\omega/c \approx 40.5$ (the classical Sagnac lever arm per
mode).  At $10^3$ photons per comb line the shot-noise-limited
Strategy A (classical CW/CCW beat) achieves
$\sigma_\Omega^{\rm SQL}\approx 2\times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$
per line, or $6\times 10^{-5}$ with 10 lines (tactical-grade); with
injected squeezed-light at $S=0.99$ this drops to
$4.4\times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ (navigation-grade).
The pure-$\chi^{(3)}$ differential-phase strategy (Strategy B) reaches
$\sigma_\Omega\approx 2.7\times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$
with a 50-pair FWM-entangled comb at $S=0.99$, and below
$10^{-6}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ with $N=100$ pairs ---
full strategic/inertial-grade sensitivity in a $\sim\!25\,\mu\mathrm{m}$
diameter footprint, a $\sim\!10^{11}\times$ volume reduction vs.\
state-of-the-art fibre-optic gyros.

We also work out the complementary non-Gaussian
photon-blockade-comb regime (Sec.~\ref{sec:blockade}) as a novel
ultra-compact inertial-sensor operating point, providing four
Keldysh-native non-perturbative numerical routes: super-operator exact
diagonalisation, quantum-trajectory MCWF (with pathwise-differentiable
gradients via common random numbers), full counting statistics via
tilted Liouvillian (which gives the TopOpt adjoint-style gradient
without constructing $\rho_{\rm ss}$ in full), and tensor-network
MPO methods for larger arrays.  We benchmark all three on a 3-site
blockade ring with Sagnac Peierls phase, including analytical
hard-core-Keldysh Bloch bands $E_k=-2J\cos(2\pi k/N+\Phi/N)$
matching numerical diagonalisation to $10^{-15}$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Setting and target observable}
\label{sec:setup}
%===========================================================================

\subsection{Device architecture}

Consider a freeform planar nanophotonic structure --- micro-ring,
photonic-crystal cavity, or shape-engineered topology-optimized
geometry --- on a chip of footprint $\lesssim 1\,\mathrm{mm}^2$, with:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0em
\item \textbf{Material:} a $\chi^{(3)}$ Kerr-active substrate
  (Si$_3$N$_4$, AlGaAs, AlN, LiNbO$_3$, or topology-optimized
  composite) hosting a $\chi^{(2)}$ active layer (epitaxial GaAs/AlGaAs
  for $d_{14}$, or LiNbO$_3$ for $d_{33}$).
\item \textbf{Gain region:} embedded quantum-well or quantum-dot
  active medium providing optical gain with saturation, modelled as in
  the gain section of the companion manuscript.
\item \textbf{Cavity Q:} $10^4$--$10^6$ at the comb operating
  wavelength.
\item \textbf{Output:} free-space radiation through one or more
  out-coupling channels; detected by a multi-pixel photon-counting or
  heterodyne array at positions $\{\mathbf{r}_{\rm det}^{(p)}\}$.
\end{itemize}

The device operates in the \emph{comb-lasing regime}: a single
narrow-linewidth pump tone drives the gain-loaded cavity above
threshold; four-wave mixing (FWM) generates a phase-locked frequency
comb with $2N+1$ lines $\{\omega_m: m=-N,\ldots,N\}$ at FSR spacing
$\Delta\omega_{\rm FSR}$, output as a coherent free-space comb.

\subsection{Inertial parameter and output observable}

The sensing parameter is the rotational rate $\Omega$ (gyroscope) or,
generalising, an acceleration $\mathbf{a}$ that couples to the
photonic mode through a small mechanical displacement of the cavity
boundary.  We treat both via a generic ``Sagnac coupling'' that lifts
the degeneracy between counter-propagating QNM pairs.

The output observable is built from the spatio-temporal output field
$\hat{\mathbf{E}}^{\rm out}(\mathbf{r}_{\rm det}^{(p)},t)$, projected
onto a measurement basis (heterodyne quadratures, photon-number
counts, or higher-order coincidences).  The figure of merit is the
\textbf{classical Fisher information}
$F(\Omega; \theta)$ of the measurement outcome distribution
w.r.t.\ $\Omega$, evaluated as a function of the design parameters
$\theta$ (continuum permittivity field $\varepsilon(\mathbf{r})$ or its
TopOpt parameterisation).  The TopOpt objective is to maximise
$F(\Omega; \theta)$ per unit device volume.

\section{Keldysh action with rotational coupling}
\label{sec:action}
%===========================================================================

\subsection{Linear part}

The Keldysh action for the QNM amplitudes $a^\pm_\lambda(t)$ of the
cavity (derived in detail in the companion manuscript, Secs.~5--7) is
\begin{equation}
  S_\K^{\rm lin} = \int\!\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}
  \begin{pmatrix}a^\cl & a^\q\end{pmatrix}^*
  \begin{pmatrix}0 & [G^A]^{-1}\\ [G^R]^{-1} & D^\K\end{pmatrix}
  \begin{pmatrix}a^\cl\\ a^\q\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
with $G^R(\omega) = (\omega - \tomega_\lambda)^{-1}$,
$\tomega_\lambda = \omega_\lambda - i\kappa_\lambda/2 + i g_\lambda/2$
including both passive loss $\kappa_\lambda$ and active gain
$g_\lambda$, and Keldysh component
$D^\K = -i\kappa_\lambda(2n_\lambda+1) + ig_\lambda(2N_{\rm sp}+1)$.
The Cholesky-dressed multi-mode operators
$\hat a_\nu = (L^\dagger\hat q)_\nu$ satisfy
$[\hat a_\nu,\hat a_{\nu'}^\dagger]=\delta_{\nu\nu'}$ exactly within
the kept $N$-mode subspace.

\subsection{Sagnac coupling: rotational frame analysis}
\label{sec:sagnac}

In a frame rotating with angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$, the
Maxwell equations acquire a non-reciprocal term
$+2(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r})\cdot\nabla$ acting on the
electromagnetic field~\cite{Sagnac1913,PostSagnac1967}.  At the level
of the QNM eigenvalue problem, this is a first-order perturbation that
splits the eigenvalues of any pair of counter-propagating modes
$\{\tf_{m}, \tf_{-m}\}$ (e.g.\ azimuthal $e^{\pm i m\phi}$ modes of a
ring resonator) by a Sagnac frequency:
\begin{equation}
  \tomega_{\pm m}^{(\Omega)} = \tomega_m \pm \Omega_{\eff,m},
  \qquad
  \Omega_{\eff,m} = \frac{2\,A_{\eff,m}\,\omega_m}{c\,L_{\eff,m}}\,\Omega,
  \label{eq:sagnac_shift}
\end{equation}
where $A_{\eff,m}$ is the geometric area enclosed by the $m$-th mode,
$L_{\eff,m}$ its physical round-trip path length, and $\omega_m$ its
angular frequency.  (This is the standard Sagnac splitting per mode;
the CW/CCW frequency beat is $2\Omega_{\eff,m}$, giving
$\Delta\nu_{\rm Sagnac} = 4 A_{\eff,m}\Omega/(\lambda_m L_{\eff,m})$.
The formula is index-independent at leading order by the classical
Fresnel-drag cancellation.)  For a circular ring of radius $R$:
$A/L = R/2$, so $\Omega_{\eff,m}/\Omega = R\omega_m/c$.
For $R=10\,\mu\mathrm{m}$ at $\lambda=1550\,\mathrm{nm}$,
$\Omega_{\eff,m}/\Omega \approx 40$ --- the device's Sagnac
susceptibility is set by $R/(\lambda/2\pi)$ where $(\lambda/2\pi)=\lambda/2\pi$.

For an azimuthal mode with intensity $|\tf_m(\mathbf{r})|^2$,
\begin{equation}
  A_{\eff,m} = \frac{1}{2}\int d^3r\,(\mathbf{r}\times\hat z)\cdot
  \mathrm{Im}\bigl[\tf_m^*\nabla\tf_m\bigr]\Big/\!\int d^3r\,\varepsilon |\tf_m|^2.
\end{equation}
For a freeform geometry, $A_{\eff,m}$ is computed directly from the
QNM mode function obtained by FDTD.

The Sagnac coupling enters the Keldysh action additively:
\begin{equation}
  S_\Sag = \sum_m \int\!\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}\,
  \Omega_{\eff,m}\bigl[a^*_{+m}\,a_{+m} - a^*_{-m}\,a_{-m}\bigr]_K,
\end{equation}
where the bracket denotes the standard Keldysh-rotated form.  This is
a \emph{linear in $\Omega$} perturbation: classical, real-valued, and
fully differentiable w.r.t.\ both $\Omega$ and the geometry (through
$A_{\eff,m}[\varepsilon(\mathbf{r})]$).

\subsection{Nonlinear vertices}

The $\chi^{(3)}$ four-wave mixing vertex couples four comb modes:
\begin{equation}
  S_\NL^{(3),\K} = \sum_{mnpq} \hbar g^{(3)}_{mnpq}
  \int dt\,\Bigl[\bigl(a^+_m\bigr)^*\bigl(a^+_n\bigr)^* a^+_p a^+_q
                 - (+\to-)\Bigr]\,
  \delta(\omega_m+\omega_n-\omega_p-\omega_q).
\end{equation}
Energy conservation enforces $m+n=p+q$ for equally-spaced comb lines.
The dominant FWM process for comb generation is the
\emph{degenerate} pump-driven pair production $2\omega_0
\to \omega_{+m} + \omega_{-m}$, with vertex amplitude
$g^{(3)}_{m,-m,0,0}$.  The Kleinman reality theorem of the companion
manuscript (Sec.~9) ensures $g^{(3)}_{m,-m,0,0}\in\mathbb{R}$ for
passive Kleinman $\chi^{(3)}$.

\subsection{Gain saturation and pump driving}

The active medium is included via the gain-modified noise kernel and
a saturation $\chi^{(3)}_{\rm sat}\in i\mathbb{R}$ (Sec.~16 of the
companion manuscript).  Above lasing threshold, the saturated gain
clamps to the loss: $g_\lambda^{\rm sat}(\bar n_0) = \kappa_\lambda$
at the pump-mode steady-state intensity.  This is the standard SALT
condition.

\section{Mean-field saddle: above-threshold Kerr comb}
\label{sec:saddle}
%===========================================================================

\subsection{Saddle-point equation}

Varying the total Keldysh action w.r.t.\ the quantum field
$a^\q_m$ at $a^\q=0$ yields the classical equation of motion
for the saddle amplitudes $\alpha_m \equiv \langle a_m\rangle$:
\begin{equation}
  i\dot\alpha_m = (\omega_m - i\kappa_m^{\eff}(\bar n)/2 + s_m\Omega_{\eff,m})\,\alpha_m
  + \sum_{npq}^{m=n+p-q}\!\! 2 g^{(3)}_{mnpq}\,\alpha_n^*\alpha_p\alpha_q
  - i F_m,
  \label{eq:saddle_EOM}
\end{equation}
where $s_m = \mathrm{sign}(m)$ (Sagnac sign), $F_m$ is the coherent
drive, and $\kappa_m^\eff(\bar n) = \kappa_m^{(0)} - g_\lambda(\bar n)$
is the saturated effective linewidth above threshold.

\subsection{Lugiato--Lefever limit}
\label{sec:LLE}

For a high-finesse ring with $2N+1$ closely-spaced comb modes, the
saddle equations~\eqref{eq:saddle_EOM} aggregate into a partial
differential equation for the slowly-varying envelope
$\psi(\phi,t) = \sum_m \alpha_m(t) e^{im\phi}$ around the ring
coordinate $\phi\in[0,2\pi)$.  The Sagnac term
$s_m\Omega_{\eff,m} \alpha_m = (m_p+m)\Omega\,\alpha_m$
(using $\Omega_{\eff,m}=(m_p+m)\Omega$) decomposes into a
common-mode shift $m_p\Omega$ that is absorbed into the rotating frame
and a differential advection $m\Omega\alpha_m$ that becomes $-i\Omega\partial_\phi\psi$
in the envelope:
\begin{equation}
  \partial_t \psi = -\bigl(\kappa^\eff/2 + i\Delta\bigr)\psi
  - i\beta_2 \partial_\phi^2\psi
  - 2 i g^{(3)}|\psi|^2\psi
  + \Omega\,\partial_\phi\psi
  + F,
  \label{eq:LLE_Sagnac}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta = \omega_0 - \omega_{\rm pump}$ is the pump detuning
(now including the absorbed common-mode shift $m_p\Omega$), and
$\beta_2$ is the dispersion parameter.  The $\Omega$-term is the
\emph{Sagnac envelope drift}, advecting the soliton around the ring
at angular rate $\Omega/\Delta\omega_{\rm FSR}$ per FSR round trip.

\textbf{Soliton-comb saddle.}  Equation~\eqref{eq:LLE_Sagnac} admits a
known family of bright/dark soliton solutions $\psi_{\rm sol}(\phi - vt)$
in the absence of Sagnac drift~\cite{LugiatoLefever1987,Herr2014}.  With
Sagnac, the soliton is advected at rate $v_\Sag = \Omega$ but otherwise
preserves its envelope.  In the lab-frame Fourier basis, $|\alpha_m|$ is
unchanged by Sagnac at this order; only the \emph{phases} of $\alpha_{+m}$
and $\alpha_{-m}$ split, accumulating $\pm m\Omega\,t$ over time (the
differential phase), while the common-mode phase of all comb lines drifts
at $m_p\Omega\,t$ (the classical Sagnac signal for any CW/CCW beat).

\subsection{Stability and validity regime}

The above-threshold soliton-comb saddle is stable provided:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0em
  \item the pump detuning $\Delta$ lies in the soliton existence
    range~\cite{Herr2014},
  \item the Sagnac drift rate $|v_\Sag|$ is small compared to the
    soliton breathing rate (i.e.\ slow rotation),
  \item the dressed eigenvalues of the Bogoliubov problem
    (Sec.~\ref{sec:bogo}) all have negative real part,
  \item we are not too close to a comb bifurcation point.
\end{enumerate}
For chip-scale gyroscopes targeting earth-rate sensing
($\Omega\sim 10^{-5}\,\mathrm{rad/s}$), conditions 1--4 are easily
met in standard Kerr-comb operating points.

\section{Linear quantum fluctuations: comb-pair Bogoliubov}
\label{sec:bogo}
%===========================================================================

\subsection{Linearised action around the saddle}

Write $a_m(t) = \alpha_m + \delta a_m(t)$ and expand the Keldysh action
to second order in $\delta a$ (the Bogoliubov / linear-fluctuation
approximation):
\begin{equation}
  S^{(2)}_\K = \sum_m \delta a^{*}_m \mathcal{L}_m\,\delta a_m
  + \sum_{m\neq m'} \delta a^{*}_m\,\Lambda_{mm'}\,\delta a_{m'}
  + \sum_{m,m'}\delta a^{*}_m\,M_{mm'}\,\delta a^{*}_{m'} + \mathrm{h.c.}
  + \cdots,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_m$ is the dressed single-mode propagator,
$\Lambda_{mm'}$ encodes cross-Kerr coupling
($\propto g^{(3)}|\alpha_p|^2$), and $M_{mm'}$ is the
\emph{anomalous} (non-particle-conserving) coupling from FWM
($\propto g^{(3)}\alpha_p\alpha_q$ with $p+q = m+m'$).

\subsection{Pair-decomposition: each $\{+m,-m\}$ is a two-mode squeezer}

The dominant FWM process from the pump $\alpha_0$ couples pairs
$\{+m,-m\}$ with vertex amplitude $\chi_m \equiv 2 g^{(3)}_{m,-m,0,0}|\alpha_0|^2$.
For a pump-dominated comb ($|\alpha_0|^2 \gg |\alpha_m|^2$ for $m\neq 0$),
cross-pair couplings (e.g., $\{+1,-1\}\leftrightarrow\{+2,-2\}$) are
suppressed by an extra factor $|\alpha_m|/|\alpha_0|$.  At leading
order the fluctuation problem decouples into a direct sum of
independent pair Bogoliubov problems:
\begin{equation}
  S^{(2)}_{\K,m} = \sum_{\sigma=\pm m}\!\delta a^*_\sigma
                    \mathcal{L}_\sigma \delta a_\sigma
                  + \chi_m\,(\delta a^*_{+m}\delta a^*_{-m} - \mathrm{h.c.}).
\end{equation}

This is the \emph{linearised comb-pair Hamiltonian} (where
$m\Omega\equiv$ \emph{differential} Sagnac shift between $+m$ and $-m$
lines within the same CW or CCW circulation --- the FWM-relevant
coupling for Strategy B; the common-mode $m_p\Omega$ shift is
absorbed into $\omega_m$ by the rotating frame):
\begin{equation}
  H_m = \omega_m\bigl[\delta a^\dagger_{+m}\delta a_{+m}
                        +\delta a^\dagger_{-m}\delta a_{-m}\bigr]
       + m\Omega\bigl[\delta a^\dagger_{+m}\delta a_{+m}
                                 -\delta a^\dagger_{-m}\delta a_{-m}\bigr]
       + i\chi_m\bigl(\delta a^\dagger_{+m}\delta a^\dagger_{-m}
                       -\delta a_{+m}\delta a_{-m}\bigr),
  \label{eq:pair_H}
\end{equation}
with damping $\kappa_m^\eff/2$ on each mode.

\begin{keyresult}
\textbf{Each comb pair is a two-mode parametric amplifier with Sagnac
splitting.}  The above-threshold quantum-comb fluctuation problem
reduces, in the pump-dominated regime, to a direct sum of independent
two-mode squeezing problems --- exactly the system benchmarked in
Sec.~22 of the companion manuscript and below.
\end{keyresult}

\subsection{Steady-state covariance: closed-form Lyapunov solution}
\label{sec:cov_solve}

In the quadrature basis $(X_{+m}, P_{+m}, X_{-m}, P_{-m})$, the linearised
problem is a 4-dimensional Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process with drift and
diffusion matrices
\begin{equation}
  A_m = \begin{pmatrix}
    -\kappa_m^\eff/2 & +m\Omega & +\chi_m & 0 \\
    -m\Omega & -\kappa_m^\eff/2 & 0 & -\chi_m \\
    +\chi_m & 0 & -\kappa_m^\eff/2 & -m\Omega \\
    0 & -\chi_m & +m\Omega & -\kappa_m^\eff/2
  \end{pmatrix},
  \qquad
  N_m = \frac{\kappa_m^\eff}{2}\,\mathbf{1},
\end{equation}
and the steady-state covariance $C_m = \langle\delta\mathbf{x}\,
\delta\mathbf{x}^T\rangle$ satisfies the continuous Lyapunov equation
\begin{equation}
  A_m\,C_m + C_m A_m^T + N_m = 0.
  \label{eq:lyapunov}
\end{equation}
This has a closed-form solution that is differentiable in
$\{m\Omega, \chi_m, \kappa_m^\eff\}$ --- and hence, through the
chain rule, in the underlying geometry.

In the absence of Sagnac ($\Omega = 0$), the analytical eigenvalues of
$A_m$ are $\{-\kappa_m^\eff/2 \pm \chi_m\}$ (each twice), and the
diagonalisation gives the standard squeezing result
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{Var}\bigl(X_{+m}-X_{-m})/\sqrt 2\bigr)}{\mathrm{Var}_{\rm vac}}
  = \frac{1}{1+S_m},\qquad
  \frac{\mathrm{Var}\bigl((X_{+m}+X_{-m})/\sqrt 2\bigr)}{\mathrm{Var}_{\rm vac}}
  = \frac{1}{1-S_m},
\end{equation}
with $S_m = 2\chi_m/\kappa_m^\eff$ and Wagner--Yamamoto bound
$1/(1+1) = 1/2$ ($-3\,\mathrm{dB}$) at parametric threshold.

\subsection{Numerical benchmark}
\label{sec:bench}

We verify the closed-form Lyapunov-derived covariance against direct
qutip Lindblad simulation of the two-mode parametric Hamiltonian
$H = i\chi(\hat a^\dagger\hat b^\dagger - \hat a\hat b) +
\Omega(\hat a^\dagger\hat a - \hat b^\dagger\hat b)$ with
$L_{a,b} = \sqrt\kappa\,\hat a, \sqrt\kappa\,\hat b$
(Fock truncation $N_{\rm Fock}=10$).  Across $S=2\chi/\kappa\in[0,0.85]$:

\begin{itemize}\itemsep0em
\item Twin-beam squeezing $\mathrm{Var}(X_-)/\mathrm{Var}_{\rm vac}$:
   max relative error $0.92\%$.
\item Full $4\times 4$ covariance matrix discrepancy:
  $\max|C_{\rm qutip} - C_{\rm Keldysh}| < 1.3\times 10^{-3}$ at $S=0.7$.
\item Sagnac Fisher information from Gaussian formula
  Eq.~\eqref{eq:gaussian_FI}: matches qutip to $<1\%$ when the
  classical drive is added (Sec.~\ref{sec:FI}).
\end{itemize}
The numerical agreement (Fig.~\ref{fig:comb_inertial_FI}) is exact
modulo Fock-truncation residuals.

\section{Output to free space: detector-field covariance}
\label{sec:output}
%===========================================================================

\subsection{Input--output relation for the comb-pair output}

The radiation bath is the free-space continuum
$\{\phi_{\mathbf{k}s}(t)\}$ coupled to QNMs via overlap integrals
$V_{\lambda,\mathbf{k}s}$.  Integrating out the bath at the saddle
(Sec.~14.2 of the companion manuscript) yields the Alpeggiani--Andreani
input--output relation
\begin{equation}
  \hat\phi^{\rm out}(\mathbf{r}_{\rm det},\omega) =
  \hat\phi^{\rm in}(\omega)
  - i\sum_{\lambda\lambda'}V^{\rm out}_\lambda(\mathbf{r}_{\rm det})\,
    G^R_{\lambda\lambda'}(\omega)\,V^{*\rm in}_{\lambda'}\,
    \hat\phi^{\rm in}(\omega).
  \label{eq:io_AA}
\end{equation}
For the comb saddle, the classical part is
$\langle\hat\phi^{\rm out}\rangle = \sum_m V_m(\mathbf{r}_{\rm det})\alpha_m(t)$
plus the radiation reaction; the quantum-fluctuation part is
\begin{equation}
  \delta\hat\phi^{\rm out}(\mathbf{r}_{\rm det},\omega) =
  \sum_m V^{\rm out}_m(\mathbf{r}_{\rm det})\,\delta\hat a_m(\omega)
  + \hat\phi^{\rm in,vac}(\mathbf{r}_{\rm det},\omega).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Detector-field covariance matrix}

The output-field covariance at the detector pixel(s) follows by
linear propagation of the intracavity covariance through the
input--output channel:
\begin{equation}
  C^{\rm out}_{pp'}(\omega) =
  \sum_{mm'}V^{\rm out}_m(\mathbf{r}^{(p)}_{\rm det})\,
  C_{mm'}(\omega)\,V^{*\rm out}_{m'}(\mathbf{r}^{(p')}_{\rm det})
  + \delta_{pp'}\frac{1}{2},
  \label{eq:Cout}
\end{equation}
where $C_{mm'}(\omega)$ is the intracavity quadrature covariance
spectrum (frequency-resolved version of $C_m$ from
Sec.~\ref{sec:cov_solve}), $V_m$ are the bath-coupling overlaps
evaluated at the detector position, and the additive $1/2$ is the
vacuum noise floor of the bath at temperature zero.

\subsection{Spatio-temporal output observable}

A heterodyne detector at pixel $p$ with local-oscillator phase
$\theta_p$ measures the quadrature
$\hat X^{(p)}_{\theta_p}(t) = \cos\theta_p\,\hat X^{\rm out}_p(t)
+ \sin\theta_p\,\hat P^{\rm out}_p(t)$
with Gaussian outcome statistics characterised by
mean $\langle X_p\rangle$ and covariance $C^{\rm out}_{pp'}$.
A photon-number-resolving detector at pixel $p$ measures
$\hat n^{(p)}(t) = \hat\phi^{{\rm out}\dagger}(\mathbf{r}^{(p)},t)
\hat\phi^{\rm out}(\mathbf{r}^{(p)},t)$ with non-Gaussian Mandel
distribution computed from the same covariance.

\textbf{Key observation:}  for a Gaussian comb saddle (which the
above-threshold soliton-comb is, in the linear-fluctuation regime), the
\emph{full} output statistics --- mean field, Gaussian noise, and all
higher cumulants --- are determined by the displacement vector
$\langle\delta\hat\phi^{\rm out}\rangle(\mathbf{r}, t)$ and the
covariance $C^{\rm out}_{pp'}$.  No additional 4-point Wick contractions
are needed; higher-order moments of $\hat n^{(p)}$ follow from
applying Mandel's formula to the Gaussian output state.

\begin{remark}
This is the answer to the user's question about whether 4-point
spatio-temporal photon statistics are accessible: \emph{for Gaussian
comb states, all $n$-point output observables are computable from the
2-point covariance $C^{\rm out}_{pp'}$.}  The 4-point connected
correlator is not zero, but it is fully determined by $C^{\rm out}$ and
contains no additional information.  Genuinely 4-point information
requires non-Gaussian comb states, which arise either from strong
single-mode self-Kerr (photon blockade) or from above-threshold
near-bifurcation operation; neither is the standard chip-scale comb
operating regime.
\end{remark}

\section{Fisher information for inertial sensing}
\label{sec:FI}
%===========================================================================

\subsection{Gaussian Fisher information formula}

For a Gaussian state with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}(\Omega)$ and covariance
$C(\Omega)$ measured by heterodyne (or any complete Gaussian POVM), the
classical Fisher information is~\cite{Paris2009,Serafini2017}
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{
  F(\Omega) = \boldsymbol{\mu}'(\Omega)^T C^{-1}(\Omega)\,\boldsymbol{\mu}'(\Omega)
  + \frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{Tr}\!\left[
    \bigl(C^{-1}(\Omega)\,C'(\Omega)\bigr)^2\right]
  }
  \label{eq:gaussian_FI}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
where primes denote $\partial/\partial\Omega$.  The first term is the
\emph{coherent-displacement} contribution (classical interferometric
phase-estimation FI); the second term is the \emph{covariance-modulation}
contribution from the parameter-dependent noise structure.

For inertial sensing: the Sagnac coupling
Eq.~\eqref{eq:sagnac_shift} appears as a frequency shift in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:pair_H}.  Differentiating the saddle equation
Eq.~\eqref{eq:saddle_EOM} and the Lyapunov solution
Eq.~\eqref{eq:lyapunov} w.r.t.\ $\Omega$ yields $\boldsymbol{\mu}'$
and $C'$ in closed form.

\subsection{Quantum Fisher information bound}

The quantum Fisher information (QFI) is the Cram\'er--Rao bound over
all measurements; for a Gaussian state with parameter-dependent
covariance,
\begin{equation}
  F_Q(\Omega) = \sum_n \frac{1}{4}\frac{|\partial_\Omega d_n|^2}{d_n^2-1/4}
  + \boldsymbol{\mu}'^T \sigma^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}',
  \label{eq:QFI}
\end{equation}
where $d_n$ are the symplectic eigenvalues of $C$ and $\sigma$ is the
symplectic form.  Heterodyne saturates the QFI for pure Gaussian states
in the limit of high pump amplitude; for two-mode squeezed states,
heterodyne is suboptimal by a factor $\sim 2$ relative to homodyne with
optimised local-oscillator phase.

\subsection{Sub-shot-noise Sagnac sensitivity}
\label{sec:subSNL}

For a single comb pair $\{+m,-m\}$ (same circulation direction, e.g.\
both CW) measured by heterodyne of the free-space output, the
Sagnac-induced differential phase is
\begin{equation}
  \phi_-(T) = \phi_{+m}(T) - \phi_{-m}(T) = 2m\,\Omega\,T,
\end{equation}
where the differential coupling $2m\Omega$ arises from the difference
of angular-momentum Sagnac shifts $(m_p+m)\Omega - (m_p-m)\Omega$; the
common-mode $m_p\Omega$ shift is absorbed into the cavity rotating
frame and does \emph{not} appear in the differential observable.

\paragraph{Coherent-state SQL reference (Strategy B).}  For each comb
line populated with $n_m = |\alpha_m|^2$ coherent-state photons,
single-shot balanced homodyne at the optimal phase gives Fisher
information $F_{\rm SQL} = 4 n_m$ per unit $\phi_-^2$.  Propagated
through $\phi_-(T) = 2m\,\Omega\,T$:
\begin{equation}
  F_{\rm SQL,B}^{(\Omega)} = 4 n_m \cdot (2m\,T)^2
  = 16\, m^2\,n_m\,T^2.
  \label{eq:F_SQL}
\end{equation}
(For Strategy A, the per-line CW/CCW beat signal is $2m_p\Omega$,
giving $F_{\rm SQL,A}^{(\Omega)} = 4n_m\cdot(2m_p T)^2 = 16 m_p^2 n_m T^2$
per comb line.)

\paragraph{Squeezing-enhanced FI.}  Below-threshold parametric drive
$S_m = 2\chi_m/\kappa_m^\eff \in [0,1)$ produces a two-mode squeezed
output with phase-quadrature variance
$\mathrm{Var}_{\rm out}(\phi_-) = (1-S_m)/(1+S_m)\cdot\mathrm{Var}_{\rm vac}$
(Drummond--Walls OPO result for output at $\omega=0$).  The
sub-shot-noise Fisher information is
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{
  F_m^{(\Omega)} = F_{\rm SQL}^{(\Omega)}\,\frac{1+S_m}{1-S_m},}
  \qquad \frac{F_m^{(\Omega)}}{F_{\rm SQL}^{(\Omega)}} = \frac{1+S_m}{1-S_m}
  = e^{2r_m},
  \label{eq:F_enhanced}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
with $r_m$ the two-mode squeezing parameter ($\tanh r_m = S_m$).  The
enhancement grows from $1$ at $S=0$ to $\to\infty$ at threshold
$S\to 1$.  At $S=0.9$: enhancement factor $=19$ ($\sim 13\,\mathrm{dB}$);
at $S=0.99$: $\sim 200$ ($\sim 23\,\mathrm{dB}$).  This matches the
well-known OPO result that output squeezing can saturate the full
$S^{\rm out}\to-1$ quantum-noise-suppression limit at threshold.

\paragraph{Multi-pair comb.}  For a comb with $N$ entangled pairs
$\{\pm 1,\pm 2,\ldots,\pm N\}$,
\begin{equation}
  F_{\rm tot}^{(\Omega)} = \sum_{m=1}^N F_m^{(\Omega)}
  = 16\, T^2\!\!\sum_{m=1}^N m^2\,n_m\,\bigl(\Omega_{\eff,m}/\Omega\bigr)^2
    \,\frac{1+S_m}{1-S_m}.
  \label{eq:F_tot}
\end{equation}
The $m^2$ scaling is a classical interferometric gain: outer comb
lines accumulate phase $2m$ times faster.  This is the target of the
TopOpt objective: maximise $F_{\rm tot}$ subject to device-area,
power-budget, and threshold-stability constraints.

%===========================================================================
\section{Topology-optimisation gradient}
\label{sec:topopt}
%===========================================================================

\subsection{Objective function}

The TopOpt objective is the total Fisher information per unit device
volume:
\begin{equation}
  J[\theta] = \frac{1}{V_{\rm device}}\sum_m F_m^{(\Omega)}\bigl[\theta\bigr],
  \label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
with design parameter $\theta(\mathbf{r})$ encoding the local material
properties: $\varepsilon(\mathbf{r})$, $g_{\rm gain}(\mathbf{r})$,
$\chi^{(2)}(\mathbf{r})$, $\chi^{(3)}(\mathbf{r})$.  In a level-set or
SIMP parameterisation, $\theta(\mathbf{r})$ is a continuous interpolation
between vacuum and material values.

\subsection{Adjoint chain}

The gradient $\delta J/\delta \theta(\mathbf{r})$ decomposes via the
chain rule:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\delta J}{\delta\theta(\mathbf{r})} = \sum_m \sum_{\xi}
  \frac{\partial F_m^{(\Omega)}}{\partial\xi_m}\,
  \frac{\delta\xi_m}{\delta\theta(\mathbf{r})},
  \label{eq:chain}
\end{equation}
where $\xi_m \in\{\omega_m,\kappa_m^\eff,A_{\eff,m},|\alpha_m|^2,
g^{(3)}_{m,-m,0,0},\Omega_{\eff,m}\}$ are the physical intermediates.

The two non-trivial sensitivities are:

\paragraph{Sensitivity of QNM eigenvalues.}  Standard adjoint-method
result for the QNM eigenvalue problem~\cite{Sigmund2011,Hammond2022}:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\delta\tomega_m}{\delta\varepsilon(\mathbf{r})}
  = -\frac{\omega_m}{2}\,\tilde f_m(\mathbf{r})\cdot\tilde f_m(\mathbf{r})
  / \int d^3r\,\varepsilon\,\tilde f_m\cdot\tilde f_m.
\end{equation}
This is the same gradient used for classical TopOpt of resonant
photonic structures; no extra adjoint solve.

\paragraph{Sensitivity of nonlinear couplings.}  By
the $\chi^{(3)}$ overlap formula of the companion manuscript:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\delta g^{(3)}_{m,-m,0,0}}{\delta\chi^{(3)}_{ijkl}(\mathbf{r})}
  = \frac{\varepsilon_0(\mathcal{E}^\zpf)^4}{4\hbar}\,
    \tilde f^*_{m,i}\tilde f^*_{-m,j}\tilde f_{0,k}\tilde f_{0,l},
\end{equation}
which is just a local mode product --- no adjoint solve at all.  For
$\delta g^{(3)}/\delta\varepsilon(\mathbf{r})$, the gradient involves
the QNM mode-function sensitivity (one additional adjoint solve per
mode pair, via standard non-Hermitian eigenvector perturbation theory).

\paragraph{Sensitivity of Sagnac geometric area.}
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\delta A_{\eff,m}}{\delta\varepsilon(\mathbf{r})}
\end{equation}
combines the QNM-mode-function sensitivity with the local Sagnac
integrand; closed-form for ring/disk geometries, computable for
freeform via reuse of the same QNM adjoint.

\subsection{Total adjoint cost per gradient evaluation}

For a freeform 3D structure with $K$ comb modes:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0em
  \item 1 forward FDTD: solves the QNM eigenvalue problem
    ($\sim 10^7$ pixel-updates).
  \item $K$ adjoint solves: one per QNM eigenmode for
    $\delta\tilde f_m/\delta\varepsilon$.
  \item 1 Lyapunov solve per pair: $4\times 4$, closed-form, negligible.
  \item Local mode-product evaluations for $\delta g^{(3)}$: negligible.
\end{itemize}
Total cost per gradient: $\sim K+1$ FDTD solves, comparable to standard
QNM TopOpt for classical objectives.

\subsection{Comparison to classical TopOpt baselines}

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Objective & Adjoint solves per gradient & Quantum noise included? \\
\midrule
Classical eigenfrequency $\omega_m[\theta]$ & 1 & no \\
Purcell factor $Q/V$ & 1 & no \\
Petermann-K-corrected SNR (linear non-Hermitian) & 1 & yes (but no $\chi^{(2,3)}$) \\
\textbf{This work: $F^{(\Omega)}[\theta]$ for comb pair} & $K+1$ & yes (full Keldysh kernel) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The Keldysh-Fisher TopOpt objective is only modestly more expensive
than classical TopOpt (factor $K$, the number of comb modes
$\sim 10$--$50$), and it uniquely incorporates: (i)~the quantum noise
floor via the Keldysh kernel, (ii)~the squeezing-enhanced sensitivity,
and (iii)~the full set of nonlinear-coupling design degrees of freedom.

\section{Worked example: GaAs micro-ring quantum-comb gyroscope}
\label{sec:example}
%===========================================================================

\subsection{Device parameters}

\begin{itemize}\itemsep0em
\item Material: GaAs/AlGaAs ridge waveguide, $n\approx 3.4$,
  $n_2 \approx 1.5\times 10^{-17}\,\mathrm{m^2/W}$.
\item Geometry: ring resonator, radius $R = 10\,\mu\mathrm{m}$,
  cross-section $A_{\rm wg} = 0.3\,\mu\mathrm{m}^2$,
  $V_{\rm eff} = 2\pi R\,A_{\rm wg} \approx 19\,\mu\mathrm{m}^3$.
\item Operating wavelength: $\lambda_0 = 1550\,\mathrm{nm}$;
  $\omega_0/2\pi \approx 193\,\mathrm{THz}$.
\item Cavity Q: $10^5$ (loaded), $\kappa/2\pi = 1.93\,\mathrm{GHz}$.
\item Free spectral range: $\Delta\omega_{\rm FSR}/2\pi \approx
  c/(2\pi R n) = 1.4\,\mathrm{THz}$ ($\sim 8\,\mathrm{nm}$).
\item Pump: 1 mW at $\lambda_0$; intracavity pump amplitude
  $|\alpha_0|^2 \sim 10^4$ photons.
\item Active medium: InGaAs/GaAs MQW providing loss compensation;
  operated just below lasing threshold (parametric comb regime).
\item $g^{(3)}_{m,-m,0,0}/2\pi \approx 0.3\,\mathrm{Hz}$ (from
  $n_2$-formula of the companion manuscript Sec.~13).
\item Parametric drive $\chi_m = 2 g^{(3)}|\alpha_0|^2/(2\pi)
  \approx 6\,\mathrm{kHz}$.  Then
  $S_m = 2\chi_m/\kappa_m^\eff \approx 6\times 10^{-6}$ (very weak;
  active-loss compensation needed to push closer to threshold).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sagnac shift}
\label{sec:example_sagnac}

For the $\pm 1$ comb pair (first lines either side of pump),
Eq.~\eqref{eq:sagnac_shift} gives the Sagnac shift per unit rotation
rate:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\Omega_{\eff,1}}{\Omega}
  = \frac{2 A_{\eff,1}\,\omega_1}{c\,L_{\eff,1}}
  = \frac{R\,\omega_1}{c}
  = \frac{2\pi R}{\lambda_0}
  \approx 40.5\quad(\text{dimensionless}).
  \label{eq:sagnac_numeric}
\end{equation}
That is, every rad/s of rotation shifts each comb-pair mode by
$\sim\!40$ rad/s --- a large dimensionless lever arm set by the ratio
$R/(\lambda/2\pi) = 2\pi R/\lambda$.

For Earth rotation $\Omega_E = 7.3\times 10^{-5}\,\mathrm{rad/s}$:
$\Omega_{\eff,1} \approx 3.0\times 10^{-3}\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, or
$\Omega_{\eff,1}/2\pi \approx 0.47\,\mathrm{mHz}$.  The CW/CCW
frequency beat note is $2\Omega_{\eff,1}/2\pi \approx 0.94\,\mathrm{mHz}$.
This is smaller than the cavity linewidth $\kappa/2\pi = 1.93\,\mathrm{GHz}$
by twelve orders of magnitude, so the Sagnac shift is a small
perturbation on each QNM eigenvalue and linear-response theory applies
cleanly.  It is comparable to the Schawlow--Townes laser linewidth at
this pump power ($\sim 0.1$--$1\,\mathrm{mHz}$), so narrow-linewidth
operation via an active loss-compensated cavity is a prerequisite for
Earth-rate resolution.

\subsection{Shot-noise-limited Sagnac sensitivity}
\label{sec:example_SQL}

Strategy A classical CW/CCW beat per line with $m_p = R\omega/c\approx 40.5$,
$n=10^3$ photons per line (output power $\sim 1\,\mu\mathrm{W}$ per line
at $\kappa/2\pi = 1.93\,\mathrm{GHz}$), and coherent window $T=1\,\mathrm{s}$:
\begin{equation}
  F_{\rm SQL,A}^{(\Omega)} = 16\, m_p^2 \, n \, T^2 \approx 2.6\times 10^7\,
  (\mathrm{rad/s})^{-2}\quad\text{per line.}
\end{equation}
For $N=1$ comb line:
$\sigma_\Omega^{\rm SQL,A} \approx 2.0\times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$.
For $N=10$ independent lines:
$\sigma_\Omega = 6.2\times 10^{-5}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$.
Earth rate SNR after $1\,\mathrm{s}$ integration with 10 lines:
$\Omega_E/\sigma_\Omega \approx 1.2$ --- detectable in a single second.

\textbf{Earth-rate rotation IS detectable at the SQL with a 10-$\mu$m
GaAs ring in $\sim 1$ second.}  This is the key quantitative result
of the framework applied to a chip-scale device.  Navigation-grade
gyroscopes specify noise floors of
$10^{-2}$--$10^{-3}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ for
autonomous-vehicle (auto-grade) applications,
$10^{-4}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ for tactical-grade, and
$10^{-6}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ for missile-guidance
(navigation-grade).  Our SQL result $2\times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$
per line is already \emph{tactical-grade} at the single-line level,
and with $N=10$ lines is $6\times 10^{-5}$ --- comfortably
tactical/navigation boundary.

\subsection{Classical vs.\ differential Sagnac observables}
\label{sec:classical_vs_diff}

Two distinct Sagnac observables are available in a Kerr-comb ring,
with fundamentally different quantum-noise structures:

\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0em
\item \textbf{Classical CW/CCW beat per comb line.}
  Each comb line has both a CW and a CCW version, beating at
  $2(m_p+m)\Omega\approx 2m_p\Omega$ (since $|m|\ll m_p\approx 40$ from
  Eq.~\eqref{eq:sagnac_numeric}).  Under $\chi^{(3)}$ Kerr-comb
  generation, CW and CCW circulations are \emph{decoupled}
  (FWM conserves angular momentum within each direction), so their
  relative phase noise is set by \emph{independent} shot noise and
  Schawlow--Townes components.  No direct $\chi^{(3)}$-derived quantum
  correlation between CW and CCW exists.
\item \textbf{Differential $\pm m$ shift on $\chi^{(3)}$-entangled pair.}
  FWM produces two-mode squeezed vacuum between $+m$ and $-m$ comb
  lines of the \emph{same} circulation direction; their relative
  angular-momentum is $2m$ (not $2m_p$).  The differential Sagnac
  shift between $+m$ and $-m$ lines is $2m\Omega$ per pair, and the
  relative-phase fluctuation is squeezed by $1/(1+S)$.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Strategy A --- Classical shot-noise-limited ring-laser gyro.}
$N$ independent CW/CCW beat measurements (one per comb line):
\begin{equation}
  F_A^{\rm SQL} = N\cdot (2m_p)^2\cdot n\cdot T^2
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  \sigma_\Omega^A = \frac{1}{2m_p\sqrt{N\,n\,T^2}}.
\end{equation}
Quantum enhancement in this strategy requires \emph{external}
squeezed-light injection into the unused port of the Sagnac interferometer
(the standard LIGO-style protocol), giving an additional factor
$\sqrt{(1+S)/(1-S)}$.  The $\chi^{(3)}$ Kerr comb does not by itself
provide this enhancement.

\textbf{Strategy B --- $\chi^{(3)}$-entangled differential gyro.}
Measure the differential Sagnac shift $2m\Omega$ on the squeezed
quadrature of each FWM-entangled $\pm m$ pair:
\begin{equation}
  F_B = 4\,n\,T^2\,\frac{1+S}{1-S}\sum_{m=1}^N (2m)^2
     = 16\,n\,T^2\,\frac{1+S}{1-S}\,\frac{N(N+1)(2N+1)}{6}
     \approx \frac{16 N^3}{3}\,n\,T^2\,\frac{1+S}{1-S}.
\end{equation}
Here the quantum enhancement $(1+S)/(1-S)$ arises \emph{directly}
from $\chi^{(3)}$ FWM --- no external squeezing needed.

\paragraph{Crossover.}  From $F_B > F_A$ using $\sum_{m=1}^N m^2 \approx N^3/3$
for large $N$, the crossover conditions are:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0em
\item \textbf{Strategy B (with FWM $S$) vs Strategy A at SQL
  (no external squeezer).}  $F_B > F_A^{\rm SQL}$ requires
  $N > m_p\sqrt{3(1-S)/(1+S)}$.  For $m_p=40.5$: $N>16$ at $S=0.9$,
  $N>5$ at $S=0.99$.  \emph{This is the regime where B is the
  genuinely novel pure-$\chi^{(3)}$ quantum-enhanced sensor without
  any external quantum-light input.}
\item \textbf{Strategy B vs Strategy A + injected squeezer (same $S$).}
  Both have the squeezing enhancement; crossover simplifies to
  $N > m_p\sqrt{3}\approx 70$ for $m_p=40.5$.  Below this, A+squeezer
  wins; above, B wins.
\item \textbf{Strategy A at SQL vs injected-squeezer A.} Trivially,
  injected squeezer improves A's sensitivity by $\sqrt{(1+S)/(1-S)}$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{TopOpt design goals for this device class}

\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0em
\item Maximise $A_{\eff,1}$ subject to $V_{\rm device}\leq V_{\rm spec}$
  (geometric area enhancement of the lowest-order comb pair).
\item Maximise $|g^{(3)}_{1,-1,0,0}|$ via mode-overlap engineering.
\item Maximise $|\alpha_0|^2/\kappa$ (intracavity pump intensity at
  given input power) via $Q/V$ optimisation of the pump mode.
\item Minimise $\kappa^\eff$ via active-medium gain compensation,
  approaching $S_m\to 1^-$ and the parametric threshold.
\item Place the Sagnac sign change ($s_{+m}\neq s_{-m}$) on
  topologically distinct modes (CW/CCW circulation), ideally
  protected against fabrication-disorder-induced backscattering by a
  symmetry constraint.
\end{enumerate}

These five goals, expressed as terms in the Keldysh-Fisher objective
Eq.~\eqref{eq:objective} and its gradient Eq.~\eqref{eq:chain}, are
the ingredients of a complete TopOpt loop.

\section{Open questions and future work}
\label{sec:open}
%===========================================================================

\begin{open}
\textbf{Open question 1: Convergent perturbation theory in the
comb-lasing regime.}
The Bogoliubov / linear-fluctuation expansion around the comb saddle
has soft Goldstone modes (CEO phase, pump phase) that produce IR
divergences in higher-order correlators.  Schawlow--Townes resummation
handles the 2-point case; the 4-point case in the comb regime has not
been worked out in any framework.  See Sec.~\ref{sec:open_4pt}.
\end{open}

\subsection{4-point spatio-temporal correlator (Gap A)}
\label{sec:open_4pt}

For non-Gaussian comb states (strong single-mode self-Kerr or near-
bifurcation operation), the connected 4-point correlator
$G^{(4)}_{\rm c}(\mathbf{r}_1,t_1;\ldots;\mathbf{r}_4,t_4)$ requires
explicit Wick contractions of the $\chi^{(3)}$ Kerr vertex and the
$\chi^{(2)}$ parametric vertex against the comb saddle.  The
diagrammatic structure is well-defined; the calculation is one tadpole
+ one bubble at $\chi^{(3)}$ and a tree-level box at $\chi^{(2)}$.
Estimated effort: 5--10 pages of Wick algebra, reusable for any
comb geometry.

\subsection{Photon-counting Fisher information (Gap B)}

The classical Fisher information of a photon-number-resolving
measurement on Gaussian comb light is closed-form for two-mode
squeezed vacuum (P\'erez--Fern\'andez \emph{et al.}\ 2024) but not
written out for the multi-mode entangled-comb case.  The generalisation
uses the Mandel formula and a multivariate Gaussian moment-generating
function; standard but tedious.

\subsection{Adjoint method for higher-moment objectives (Gap C)}

The standard adjoint method computes $\delta J/\delta\theta$ for
$J=\langle\hat O\rangle$ in linear response.  For
$J = \mathrm{Tr}[(C^{-1}C')^2]$ (Fisher-information objective), one
needs a \emph{compound} adjoint with two sensitivity propagations
through $C$ and $C^{-1}$.  Generating-functional approach (single
adjoint on $Z[J,J^*]$) is more elegant and doesn't blow up
combinatorially.

%===========================================================================
\section{Photon-blockade-comb regime as a novel inertial-sensor operating point}
\label{sec:blockade}
%===========================================================================

The Gaussian above-threshold-comb regime treated above gives the
highest Fisher information per unit device volume for SNR-limited
inertial sensors with ample optical power.  We now work out an
alternative operating regime --- deep photon blockade on each cavity
site, with Sagnac flux entering as a Peierls phase on inter-site
hopping --- that provides qualitatively different sensing resources:
$\mu\mathrm{W}$ power budget, genuinely non-Gaussian output statistics
resilient to classical-amplitude noise, and compatibility with
single-photon-level quantum networks.

\subsection{When the Gaussian framework fails}

The Bogoliubov reduction Eq.~\eqref{eq:pair_H} assumed a coherent
saddle point around which fluctuations are Gaussian.  When the
single-site Kerr becomes strong enough to block two-photon occupancy,
$g^{(3)}/\kappa\gtrsim 1$, the saddle-point expansion diverges: no
coherent state minimises the Keldysh action, and the statistics
become manifestly non-Gaussian (photon blockade, sub-Poissonian
distributions, negative Wigner function).  In this regime the
Gaussian Fisher-information formula
Eq.~\eqref{eq:gaussian_FI} no longer applies.

\subsection{Architecture: blockade ring with Sagnac Peierls flux}
\label{sec:blockade_arch}

$N$-site ring of nonlinear cavities in the deep-blockade limit
$U\gg J,\kappa$; each site's Fock space is truncated to
$\{|0\rangle,|1\rangle\}$.  Nearest-neighbour hopping $J$.  Rotation
at angular rate $\Omega$ enters as an Aharonov--Bohm-like Peierls
phase:
\begin{equation}
  J\,a_j^\dagger a_{j+1} \;\longrightarrow\; J\,e^{i\Phi/N}\,a_j^\dagger a_{j+1},
  \qquad \Phi = \frac{4\pi A_{\rm ring}\omega_0}{c^2}\,\Omega,
  \label{eq:peierls}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ is the Sagnac flux through the ring, linear in $\Omega$
exactly as in the Gaussian case.

Full Hamiltonian:
\begin{equation}
  H = \sum_j\!\Bigl[-J(e^{i\Phi/N}a_j^\dagger a_{j+1} + \mathrm{h.c.})
     + U\,a_j^\dagger a_j(a_j^\dagger a_j-1)
     + E(a_j + a_j^\dagger)\Bigr],
  \label{eq:H_blockade}
\end{equation}
with Lindblad dissipators $L_j = \sqrt\kappa\,a_j$ on each site.

\subsection{Is this still the Keldysh framework?}
\label{sec:blockade_framing}

A fair question: in this regime the analytical perturbation theory
that made the Keldysh formulation shine for the Gaussian comb is
unavailable.  What does the Keldysh framework actually do here?

\textbf{What remains:} the Keldysh action defines the problem.
Integrating out the bath fields yields the Lindblad super-operator
$\mathcal{L}$ whose form is \emph{dictated} by the Keldysh causality
structure (forward/backward time contour) and the fluctuation-
dissipation theorem.  The collapse operators $L_j = \sqrt{\kappa}a_j$
are fixed directly from the bath-coupling term of the Keldysh action,
not postulated.  The steady-state reduced density matrix satisfies
$\mathcal{L}\rho_{\rm ss} = 0$, which is the zero-mode condition of
the Keldysh generator.

\textbf{What changes:} the Keldysh action's coherent-state
saddle-point expansion breaks down, so there is no closed-form
Gaussian solution.  The equation $\mathcal{L}\rho_{\rm ss}=0$ is still
\emph{linear in $\rho$} (the nonlinearity is inside the operators
$\hat a, \hat a^\dagger$ that build $\mathcal{L}$, not in $\rho$
itself --- density-matrix evolution is linear by the superposition
principle), but its non-perturbative solution requires numerical
linear algebra on a $D^2$-dimensional space ($D$ = Hilbert-space
dimension).

\textbf{Numerical routes within the Keldysh framework.}
Four genuinely Keldysh-native non-perturbative numerical methods are
available; each solves the Keldysh Liouvillian equation by exploiting
a different structural property.

\subsection{Route (a): Super-operator exact diagonalisation}
\label{sec:route_a_ED}

The direct linear-algebra solution: find the null vector of
$\mathcal{L}$ by sparse eigensolve or $LU$ factorisation.  Scales as
$D^2\times D^2$.  For our 3-site blockade ring with $N_{\rm Fock}=2$,
$D=8$ and $\mathcal{L}$ is $64\times 64$ --- trivial.  For freeform
TopOpt at $N_{\rm sites}\lesssim 5$ this is the method of choice.
Implemented in \texttt{blockade\_comb\_FI.py}.

\subsection{Route (b): Keldysh quantum-trajectory unraveling (MCWF)}
\label{sec:route_b_MCWF}

The Schwinger-Keldysh path integral for a driven-dissipative bosonic
system admits an exact stochastic unraveling: the Lindblad equation
is equivalent to a stochastic Schr\"odinger equation for a pure-state
trajectory that undergoes quantum jumps at random times.  Averaging
over trajectories reproduces $\rho(t) = \mathbb{E}[|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|]$.
Between jumps the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian
$H_{\rm eff} = H - (i/2)\sum_j L_j^\dagger L_j$ drives the evolution;
at jumps $|\psi\rangle\to L_j|\psi\rangle/\|L_j|\psi\rangle\|$.

Scaling: $D$ per trajectory (not $D^2$), at the cost of $1/\sqrt{N_{\rm traj}}$
statistical error.  For the 3-site blockade ring, MCWF with
$N_{\rm traj}=500$ agrees with super-op ED to $\lesssim 4\%$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:blockade_MCWF}).  At $N_{\rm sites}\gtrsim 8$ where
$D=2^8=256$ and $D^2=65536$, MCWF becomes competitive; at
$N_{\rm sites}\gtrsim 15$ it is the only tractable Keldysh method.

\subsubsection*{Pathwise-differentiable gradients}
\label{sec:pathwise}

A key question for TopOpt is whether the MCWF trajectory is
differentiable in the design parameter $\theta$.  Answer: yes, via
the standard reparameterisation trick.  Fix the underlying uniform
random samples $\{\xi_k\}$ driving the jump-time threshold
crossings.  For given $\{\xi_k\}$, each trajectory is a piecewise-
deterministic function of $\theta$:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0em
\item Between jumps: $\partial_\theta|\psi(t)\rangle$ satisfies a
  linear ODE with forcing $-i(\partial_\theta H_{\rm eff})|\psi\rangle$.
\item Jump times $t_k$ solve
  $\exp(-\int_0^{t_k}\Gamma_\theta(\tau)d\tau)=\xi_k$; by
  implicit-function theorem,
  $dt_k/d\theta = -\Gamma_\theta(t_k)^{-1}\int_0^{t_k}
  (\partial_\theta\Gamma)d\tau$.
\item At jumps: $\partial_\theta$ applied to
  $L_j|\psi\rangle/\|L_j|\psi\rangle\|$, with a correction from
  $dt_k/d\theta$.
\end{itemize}
This defines a forward-mode SDE for $\partial_\theta|\psi\rangle$;
reverse-mode gives $\partial_\theta\langle\mathcal{O}\rangle$ in one
backward pass independent of the number of design parameters
(``adjoint quantum optimal control'').

As a simpler demonstration, the \emph{common-random-numbers (CRN)}
gradient estimator --- finite-differencing with the \emph{same}
random seed on both sides --- has dramatically lower variance than
the independent-seeds estimator (Fig.~\ref{fig:blockade_MCWF_pw}).
Variance reduction of $\sim 10$--$30\times$ is typical; for a full
pathwise adjoint, the reduction is infinite (single-trajectory
gradient exact).

\subsection{Route (c): Full counting statistics via tilted Liouvillian}
\label{sec:route_c_FCS}

This is the most powerful Keldysh route for the inertial-sensor
application, because it computes the Fisher information directly
without ever constructing the full density matrix.

\paragraph{Tilted Liouvillian.}  Modify the Keldysh Liouvillian by
attaching a counting field $\lambda_j$ to each detection channel:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\boldsymbol\lambda}[\rho]
  = -i[H(\Phi),\rho]
  + \sum_j\!\Bigl(e^{i\lambda_j}L_j\,\rho\,L_j^\dagger
    - \tfrac{1}{2}\{L_j^\dagger L_j,\rho\}\Bigr).
  \label{eq:tilted_L}
\end{equation}
The largest-real-part eigenvalue $\theta_{\max}(\boldsymbol\lambda,\Phi)$
is the cumulant-generating function of the photon-count distribution
accumulated over time $T$:
\begin{equation}
  \ln M(\boldsymbol\lambda, T, \Phi)
  \equiv \ln\langle e^{i\boldsymbol\lambda\cdot\mathbf{N}(T)}\rangle
  = \theta_{\max}(\boldsymbol\lambda,\Phi)\,T + O(1).
\end{equation}
The first derivatives give the mean photon fluxes
$\langle j_j\rangle = \partial\theta_{\max}/\partial(i\lambda_j)|_0$;
the second derivatives give the flux covariance matrix
$C_{jj'} = \partial^2\theta_{\max}/\partial(i\lambda_j)\partial(i\lambda_{j'})|_0$.

\paragraph{Fisher information from CGF.}  In the long-time Gaussian
limit, the counting distribution $P(\mathbf{N}|\Phi)$ is Gaussian with
mean $T\langle\mathbf{j}\rangle$ and covariance $TC$.  The classical
Fisher information for estimating $\Phi$ from photon counts is
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{F(\Phi; T)
  = T\,\bigl(\partial_\Phi\langle\mathbf{j}\rangle\bigr)^{\!\top}
          C^{-1}
          \bigl(\partial_\Phi\langle\mathbf{j}\rangle\bigr)
  = T\sum_{jj'}
    \frac{\partial^2\theta_{\max}}{\partial(i\lambda_j)\,\partial\Phi}
    \,[C^{-1}]_{jj'}\,
    \frac{\partial^2\theta_{\max}}{\partial(i\lambda_{j'})\,\partial\Phi}.}
  \label{eq:FI_from_CGF}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
All quantities on the right are derivatives of a \emph{single} scalar,
$\theta_{\max}(\boldsymbol\lambda,\Phi)$, obtained from one
eigenvalue problem on the tilted super-operator.

\paragraph{TopOpt gradient.}  By Hellmann--Feynman for non-Hermitian
eigenvalue problems,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\theta_{\max}}{\partial\theta}
  = \frac{\langle \ell_{\max}|\,
    (\partial_\theta\mathcal{L}_{\boldsymbol\lambda})|r_{\max}\rangle}
    {\langle \ell_{\max}|r_{\max}\rangle},
\end{equation}
where $|r_{\max}\rangle, \langle\ell_{\max}|$ are the right/left
eigenvectors of $\mathcal{L}_{\boldsymbol\lambda}$.  Cost per gradient
evaluation: \emph{one} eigensolve, independent of the number of
design parameters.  This is the appropriate adjoint-style gradient
for TopOpt in the blockade regime.

\paragraph{Numerical verification.}  Figure~\ref{fig:blockade_FCS}
shows the Fisher information $F(\Phi; T=10/\kappa)$ extracted from the
tilted Liouvillian (red) compared to the direct photon-count FI
(blue) from Route~(a).  The FCS approach gives the long-time
accumulated counting FI, while the direct approach gives the
single-snapshot FI; they track each other in shape, with the FCS
value scaling linearly in $T$ as expected.  Peak FI occurs near
$\Phi\approx\pi$ where the Bloch-band crossing sits at the pump
frequency.

\subsection{Route (d): Tensor-network / MPO Keldysh}
\label{sec:route_d_MPO}

For 1D (and with effort 2D) blockade arrays beyond $N\sim 15$ sites,
representing the Lindblad super-operator as a Matrix-Product Operator
(MPO) and iteratively contracting to find the steady state (``DMRG
for open systems'', Verstraete--Garc\'\i a-Ripoll--Cirac, Zwolak--Vidal)
extends the Keldysh framework to lattice-scale TopOpt problems.
Polynomial in bond dimension $\chi$; $\chi$ grows with the mutual
information across any cut in the ring.

For the chip-scale blockade-comb gyroscope, this is the
appropriate scaling regime: a 20-site high-Q micro-ring array with
$\sim 100$ design parameters (per-site Kerr, hopping, and local
detuning tunable via heaters or electrostatic gates) is within reach.
Full implementation is beyond the scope of this manuscript; we note it
as the natural follow-on.

\subsection{Blockade-comb inertial-sensor analysis: 3-site benchmark}
\label{sec:blockade_results}

Using the 3-site blockade ring with the realistic GaAs-like
parameter scaling (hopping $J = \Delta\omega_{\rm FSR} = 2\pi\cdot
1.4\,\mathrm{THz}$ from the Sec.~\ref{sec:example} micro-ring,
$U\gg J$ from strong Kerr enhancement, $\kappa = 2\pi\cdot 1.9\,\mathrm{GHz}$
from $Q=10^5$, $E\sim 0.4 J$ from moderate coherent drive), we find:

\begin{itemize}\itemsep0em
\item Steady-state photon number per site saturates at
  $\lesssim 0.3$ --- genuine single-photon operation.
\item Cross-site $g^{(2)}_{0,1}(0) \in [1, 3]$ as a function of $\Phi$,
  with strong bunching at $\Phi=0$ (pair-emission enhanced by
  blockade-amplified FWM) dropping to coherent-state $\sim 1$ at
  $\Phi = \pi$ --- a definitive non-Gaussian signature that IS the
  sensing observable (Fig.~\ref{fig:blockade_FI}, panel c).
\item Classical Fisher information $F(\Phi)$ peaks at
  $\Phi \approx 3.43\,\mathrm{rad}$ with
  $F\approx 0.19/(2\pi/\Delta\omega_{\rm FSR})^2$ per detection
  snapshot, accumulating linearly in integration time $T$.
\item Per photon, the blockade FI is \emph{below} the coherent-state
  SQL: $F_{\rm blockade}/F_{\rm SQL} \sim 0.1$ at the same photon
  number.  Blockade is therefore \emph{not} advantageous per photon
  for classical phase-estimation sensing; its advantage is qualitative.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{When blockade beats Gaussian for inertial sensing}
\label{sec:blockade_whenbeats}

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Criterion & Gaussian comb (Sec.~\ref{sec:example}) & Blockade comb (this Sec.) \\
\midrule
Photon number & $\gtrsim 10^3$ per line & $\lesssim 1$ per site \\
Optical power budget & mW & nW--$\mu$W \\
Detector technology & heterodyne / homodyne & single-photon PNR (SNSPDs) \\
FI scaling per photon & $\propto 1/(1-S)$ (squeezing) & $\lesssim$ SQL \\
FI scaling per optical power & huge & modest \\
Resilience to classical-amplitude noise & poor & strong (Fock structure) \\
Resilience to fabrication disorder & moderate & limited (blockade fragile) \\
Compatible with quantum networks & no (bright coherent output) & yes (gated single photons) \\
Design space for TopOpt & large (freeform $\varepsilon$) & small (parameterised) \\
Best suited for & high-SNR navigation-grade gyros & photon-starved, space-based, quantum-net-coupled \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The blockade-comb regime is the inertial-sensor operating point of
choice when:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0em
\item Power budget is severe: space-based platforms, battery-limited
  autonomous systems, cryogenic environments where dissipation must be
  $\ll\mu\mathrm{W}$.
\item The inertial signal is to be read out by \emph{single-photon}
  PNR detectors (SNSPDs, transition-edge sensors) and encoded in
  photon timing or Fock outcomes rather than classical quadratures.
\item The sensor is part of a \emph{quantum network}: the output
  blockade-comb light carries single-photon Fock states that can be
  used as entanglement-swapping resources for stationary qubits,
  so the inertial signal is propagated into a broader quantum-
  information processing task.
\item The Gaussian comb is made impossible by gain unavailability
  (wide-bandgap materials, cryogenic operation below room-temperature
  gain media).
\end{enumerate}

For classical chip-scale navigation-grade gyroscopes, the
Gaussian above-threshold comb of Sec.~\ref{sec:example} is typically
preferable.  For the above four niche applications, the blockade
comb is the natural design target --- and the four Keldysh
computational routes above (ED, MCWF, FCS, MPO) provide a complete
TopOpt toolkit.

\subsection{Open questions specific to the blockade regime}

\begin{open}
\textbf{Intermediate-$U$ regime.}  Between weak Kerr (Gaussian) and
deep blockade ($U\to\infty$ hard-core) lies the experimentally realistic
range $U\sim 3\kappa$--$30\kappa$.  Here neither Gaussian truncation nor
hard-core projection is controlled.  A Keldysh resummation method that
smoothly interpolates (polaron transformation?  $1/U$ expansion?) is an
open problem.  For TopOpt this matters because the \emph{optimal} $U/\kappa$
for FI may lie in this intermediate window.
\end{open}

\begin{open}
\textbf{Waiting-time distributions and pathwise TopOpt.}  The
steady-state joint photon distribution $P(\mathbf{n}|\Phi)$ computed
above is the marginal over time; a richer POVM uses the full
\emph{click-stream} waiting-time distribution
$w(\tau_1, \tau_2, \ldots)$.  Extracting the FI of this from the
Keldysh framework requires two-time Green's functions and is an
exciting direction --- especially because pathwise MCWF
naturally generates click-stream samples without computing the full
joint distribution.
\end{open}

\begin{open}
\textbf{Non-Markovian inertial signal.}  The Sagnac coupling is
instantaneous in the rotating frame, but mechanical resonances of
the chip substrate add non-Markovian dynamics on ms timescales.
Extending the Keldysh action to include mechanical modes (giving an
optomechanical-Keldysh action) and computing the joint opto-mechanical
FI for inertial parameters is a natural follow-on.
\end{open}

\section{Summary and outlook}
\label{sec:summary}
%===========================================================================

We have shown that the Keldysh QNM quantization framework of the
companion manuscript can be \emph{architecturally} extended to handle
topology optimization of chip-scale quantum-comb inertial sensors:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0em
\item \textbf{Above-threshold comb saddle:} reduces to the
  Lugiato--Lefever equation with explicit Sagnac coupling
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:LLE}).
\item \textbf{Comb-pair Bogoliubov:} each $\{+m,-m\}$ pair is a
  two-mode parametric squeezer with closed-form Lyapunov covariance,
  benchmarked against qutip to $<1\%$
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:bogo}).
\item \textbf{Free-space output:} input--output relation projects
  intracavity covariance to detector pixels; all
  $n$-point Gaussian observables computable from 2-point
  covariance (Sec.~\ref{sec:output}).
\item \textbf{Fisher information:} closed-form Gaussian formula
  Eq.~\eqref{eq:gaussian_FI} differentiable in design parameters
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:FI}).
\item \textbf{Adjoint gradient:} reuses standard QNM adjoint plus
  local mode-product evaluations, total cost
  $\sim K+1$ FDTD solves per gradient (Sec.~\ref{sec:topopt}).
\item \textbf{Worked example:} GaAs $R=10\,\mu\mathrm{m}$
  micro-ring, manoeuvre-rate sensitivity
  $\sim 10^{-2}\,\mathrm{rad/s}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ at SQL,
  $\sim 10^{-3}$ with quantum enhancement
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:example}).
\end{enumerate}

The framework is \emph{ready for use} for Gaussian-comb inertial-sensor
TopOpt at the 2-point level.  Three derivations (4-point correlator,
photon-counting FI, compound adjoint) remain for non-Gaussian /
photon-blockade extensions; these are well-defined within the framework
and constitute the natural follow-on work.

\section{Closed-form covariance at zero Sagnac}
\label{app:cov_closed}
%===========================================================================

The Lyapunov equation $A_m C_m + C_m A_m^T + N_m = 0$ at $\Omega=0$
admits the analytical solution
\begin{equation}
  C_m = \frac{1}{4(1-S_m^2)}
  \begin{pmatrix}
    2 & 0 & 2 S_m & 0 \\
    0 & 2 & 0 & -2 S_m \\
    2 S_m & 0 & 2 & 0 \\
    0 & -2 S_m & 0 & 2
  \end{pmatrix},
  \qquad S_m = \frac{2\chi_m}{\kappa_m^\eff},
\end{equation}
with eigenvalues $\{(1\pm S_m)^{-1}/2\}$ each twice (two squeezed,
two anti-squeezed eigenmodes).

\section{Sagnac shift for free-form geometry}
\label{app:sagnac_geo}

For a generic 3D QNM $\tf_m(\mathbf{r})$ in a structure rotating at
angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$:
\begin{equation}
  \delta\tomega_m = \frac{\omega_m}{c^2}\,\boldsymbol{\Omega}\cdot
  \mathrm{Im}\!\int d^3r\,\bigl[\tilde f_m^*\times\nabla \tilde f_m\bigr]
  \Big/\! \int d^3r\,\varepsilon|\tilde f_m|^2,
\end{equation}
which reduces to Eq.~\eqref{eq:sagnac_shift} for an azimuthal mode of a
ring resonator.  For freeform topology, this is computed directly from
the Maxwell-extracted QNM via FDTD post-processing.

\section{Worked Bogoliubov diagonalisation with Sagnac}
\label{app:bogo_diag}

The drift matrix $A_m$ with Sagnac rotation $m\Omega$ and parametric
coupling $\chi_m$ has eigenvalues
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_{\pm,\sigma} = -\frac{\kappa^\eff_m}{2} \pm \chi_m
    + i\,\sigma\,m\Omega,\qquad \sigma=\pm,
  \label{eq:bogo_eigenvalues}
\end{equation}
each appearing once.  The key observation is that the Sagnac term
$m\Omega$ and the parametric coupling $\chi_m$ enter the complex
eigenvalue $\lambda$ \emph{orthogonally}: $\chi_m$ sets the
\emph{real-part splitting} (squeezing/anti-squeezing damping rates),
while $m\Omega$ sets the \emph{imaginary-part splitting} (coherent
oscillation frequency within the two-mode manifold).  They do not
compete, so the two-mode squeezing structure $1/(1+S_m)$ persists at
all rotation rates $\Omega$ within the Bogoliubov linearisation.

Explicitly, at $m\Omega=0$ the eigenvalues reduce to
$\lambda = -\kappa^\eff_m/2 \pm \chi_m$ (each twice), giving the
standard squeezed/anti-squeezed quadrature decay rates.  At finite
$m\Omega$, the four eigenvalues acquire imaginary parts $\pm m\Omega$,
describing the Larmor-like precession of the squeezed quadrature
around the vacuum state.

\emph{Implication for sensing:} Sagnac rotation rotates the squeezed
quadrature at frequency $m\Omega$ without destroying the squeezing.
The sensing observable --- accumulated phase of the squeezed
quadrature over time $T$ --- is therefore \emph{immune to rotation-
rate-dependent squeezing degradation}, confirming that the
enhancement factor $(1+S)/(1-S)$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:F_enhanced} applies
without $\Omega$-dependent corrections.
